How to Get a Better Credit Mix with a 690 Score

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

A credit score of 690 sits right on the edge of "good" and "fair," making it a critical point for financial growth. Whether you're eyeing a mortgage, a car loan, or better credit card offers, improving your credit mix can be the key to unlocking better opportunities. In today’s volatile economy—where inflation, rising interest rates, and tighter lending standards dominate headlines—having a strong credit profile is more important than ever.

Why Credit Mix Matters

Your credit mix accounts for 10% of your FICO score, and lenders use it to assess how well you handle different types of debt. A diverse credit portfolio—including revolving credit (credit cards) and installment loans (mortgages, auto loans, student loans)—shows you can manage multiple financial responsibilities.

The Current Financial Landscape

With inflation driving up costs and the Federal Reserve raising interest rates, lenders are becoming more cautious. A 690 score might get you approved, but not always at the best terms. Strengthening your credit mix can help you secure lower APRs and higher credit limits.

Steps to Improve Your Credit Mix with a 690 Score

1. Evaluate Your Current Credit Profile

Before making changes, pull your credit reports from AnnualCreditReport.com (free weekly reports until December 2023). Look for:
- Revolving accounts (credit cards, retail cards)
- Installment loans (auto, mortgage, personal loans)
- Missing categories (e.g., no installment history if you’ve only used credit cards)

2. Add a New Type of Credit Strategically

If your report lacks installment loans, consider:
- A credit-builder loan (offered by credit unions or online lenders like Self or Credit Strong)
- A small personal loan (if you can repay it quickly)
- Financing a minor purchase (e.g., furniture or electronics with a 0% APR promo)

Warning: Only take on debt you can afford. Missing payments hurts more than a thin credit mix.

3. Become an Authorized User

If you lack revolving credit, ask a family member with a long-standing, low-utilization card to add you as an authorized user. Their positive history can boost your profile—but confirm the issuer reports authorized users to credit bureaus (Amex, Chase, and Citi do).

4. Apply for a Secured Credit Card

If you’ve been denied unsecured cards, a secured card (where you deposit cash as collateral) can help. Use it for small, regular purchases and pay the balance in full. Over time, issuers like Discover or Capital One may upgrade you to an unsecured card.

5. Avoid Closing Old Accounts

Closing a credit card reduces your available credit, which can spike your utilization ratio (the % of credit you’re using). Even if you don’t use an old card, keep it open to maintain a longer credit history.

Navigating Economic Challenges

Rising Interest Rates & Your Credit Strategy

The Fed’s rate hikes mean higher APRs on loans and cards. To mitigate this:
- Refinance existing debt if possible (e.g., consolidate high-interest credit cards with a personal loan).
- Prioritize variable-rate debts (pay them down faster to avoid compounding interest).

Recession-Proofing Your Credit

Experts debate whether a 2023 recession is looming. If it hits, lenders may tighten approvals. Strengthening your credit mix now ensures you’re prepared for:
- Emergency loans (better terms if your score climbs to 700+)
- Job loss backup plans (access to low-interest credit lines)

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. Applying for Multiple Credit Lines at Once

Each hard inquiry dings your score by 5–10 points. Space out applications by 3–6 months.

2. Ignoring Credit Utilization

Even with a diverse mix, maxing out cards hurts your score. Aim for <30% utilization (ideally under 10%).

3. Overlooking Errors on Your Report

Dispute inaccuracies (e.g., late payments you didn’t make) via the CFPB’s complaint portal.

Final Tactics for Fast Improvement

  • Rent reporting services (e.g., Piñata or RentTrack) can add rental payments to your credit history.
  • Ask for higher limits on current cards (lowers utilization if spending stays the same).
  • Use Experian Boost to include utility/phone bills in your score calculation.

A 690 score is a solid foundation—optimizing your credit mix could push you into the 700s, unlocking better financial flexibility in an uncertain economy. Stay disciplined, diversify wisely, and monitor your progress monthly.
