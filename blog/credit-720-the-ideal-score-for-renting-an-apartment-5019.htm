Credit 720: The Ideal Score for Renting an Apartment

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

Why Your Credit Score Matters More Than Ever

In today’s competitive rental market, landlords and property managers rely heavily on credit scores to screen potential tenants. A score of 720 or higher is often considered the gold standard—a sweet spot that signals financial responsibility and reliability. But why has this number become so crucial, and how does it impact your ability to secure the perfect apartment?

The Rising Cost of Housing and Landlord Risk Aversion

With housing shortages in major cities like New York, Los Angeles, and San Francisco, landlords face an overwhelming number of applications for limited units. To minimize risk, they prioritize tenants with strong credit histories. A 720+ credit score suggests you pay bills on time, manage debt wisely, and are less likely to default on rent.

Inflation and economic uncertainty have also made landlords more cautious. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed vulnerabilities in rental income streams, leading to stricter screening processes. A high credit score isn’t just a number—it’s a financial passport to better housing options.

How Credit Scores Affect Rental Applications

The Landlord’s Perspective

Property owners use credit scores to:
- Predict payment reliability – A high score means you’re likely to pay rent consistently.
- Reduce eviction risks – Tenants with poor credit are statistically more likely to face financial hardships.
- Justify higher deposits – If your score is below 720, landlords may require extra security deposits.

The Tenant’s Advantage

A 720+ credit score gives you:
- Lower security deposits – Some landlords waive deposits entirely for high-scoring renters.
- Negotiation power – You can leverage your score to negotiate rent or lease terms.
- Access to premium properties – Luxury apartments often enforce strict credit requirements.

How to Achieve (and Maintain) a 720 Credit Score

1. Pay Bills on Time, Every Time

Your payment history makes up 35% of your FICO score. Even one late payment can drop your score by 50+ points. Set up autopay for credit cards, loans, and utilities to avoid missed deadlines.

2. Keep Credit Utilization Below 30%

Using too much of your available credit hurts your score. If you have a $10,000 limit, try to keep balances under $3,000. Lower utilization (ideally under 10%) boosts your score faster.

3. Avoid Opening Too Many New Accounts

Each hard inquiry from a credit application can shave 5-10 points off your score. If you’re planning to rent soon, hold off on applying for new credit cards or loans.

4. Build a Long Credit History

The average age of your accounts matters. If you’re new to credit, consider becoming an authorized user on a family member’s card or keeping old accounts open (even if unused).

5. Monitor Your Credit Reports

Errors on your report can drag down your score. Use AnnualCreditReport.com to check for inaccuracies and dispute them immediately.

What If Your Score Is Below 720?

Alternative Strategies for Renters

  • Offer a higher security deposit – Some landlords may overlook a lower score if you pay extra upfront.
  • Get a co-signer – A guarantor with strong credit can strengthen your application.
  • Provide proof of income – Bank statements or pay stubs can reassure landlords of your ability to pay.
  • Look for private landlords – Smaller property owners may be more flexible than corporate leasing offices.

The Future of Credit Checks in Renting

As AI-driven tenant screening becomes more common, credit scores may evolve to include alternative data like rental payment history (via services like RentTrack or Experian Boost). Some cities are also pushing for rental equity laws to prevent credit scores from being the sole deciding factor.

For now, though, 720 remains the magic number—a benchmark that opens doors (literally) in today’s cutthroat rental market. Whether you’re a first-time renter or a seasoned tenant, prioritizing your credit health is one of the smartest financial moves you can make.
