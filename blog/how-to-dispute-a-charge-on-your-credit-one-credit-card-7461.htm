How to Dispute a Charge on Your Credit One Credit Card

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

In today’s fast-paced digital economy, credit cards are more than just a convenience—they are a necessity. From online shopping and subscription services to global travel and contactless payments, our financial lives are increasingly conducted through plastic and digital wallets. With this convenience, however, comes risk. Unauthorized transactions, billing errors, and outright fraud are on the rise, fueled by sophisticated cybercriminals and the complexities of global supply chains. If you’re a Credit One Bank cardholder, knowing how to effectively dispute a charge is not just a skill—it’s an essential layer of financial self-defense.

Why Disputing Charges Matters More Than Ever

We live in an era of recurring subscriptions, instant digital downloads, and cross-border e-commerce. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the shift to online shopping, and with it, a surge in "friendly fraud" (where a family member makes a purchase without telling you) and merchant disputes over undelivered goods or services. Furthermore, data breaches at major retailers have become commonplace, leaving consumers' card information vulnerable. The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) received over 5.7 million fraud and identity theft reports in 2023, with credit card fraud being the most common type. Disputing an incorrect charge is your right under federal law, specifically the Fair Credit Billing Act (FCBA), and it’s a critical tool for protecting your hard-earned money and your credit score.

Before You Dispute: Immediate Steps to Take

The moment you spot a suspicious charge on your Credit One credit card statement, don’t panic. But act quickly.

1. Review the Transaction Thoroughly

First, log into your Credit One online account or mobile app. Scrutinize the charge. Often, a merchant’s name on your statement might be different from their public-facing brand name (e.g., "AMZNMktp" instead of "Amazon"). A quick online search can often clarify this. Also, check if the charge is a recurring subscription you forgot about or a pre-authorization hold from a hotel or rental car company that will fall off automatically.

2. Contact the Merchant Directly

This is a crucial and often overlooked step. If the charge is due to a misunderstanding, a product not received, or a service not rendered, the merchant might issue a refund immediately to avoid a formal dispute. This is usually the fastest way to resolve the issue. Have your receipt or order confirmation ready.

3. Update Your Contact Information

Ensure your mailing address, email, and phone number are up to date in your Credit One account. The bank will need to communicate with you throughout the dispute process, often via postal mail.

The Formal Dispute Process with Credit One Bank

If contacting the merchant fails, or if the charge is clearly fraudulent, it’s time to initiate a formal dispute with Credit One Bank.

Step 1: How to File the Dispute

Credit One offers several ways to file a dispute: * Online (Recommended): This is the fastest method. Log into your account, navigate to the disputed transaction, and look for an option like "Dispute this Charge" or "Question a Transaction." You’ll be guided through a digital process to submit your claim. * Via Mobile App: The process is similar to the online portal. The app provides a convenient way to monitor your dispute’s status on the go. * By Phone: Call the number on the back of your credit card. Be prepared for potentially long wait times. Have your account information and details about the transaction ready. * By Mail: While slower, you can send a written dispute letter to Credit One Bank. Send it via certified mail with a return receipt requested to have proof of delivery. Your letter must include your name, account number, the date and amount of the disputed charge, and a detailed explanation of why you are disputing it.

Step 2: What Information You'll Need to Provide

To build a strong case, gather your evidence. The burden of proof often lies with you, the cardholder. Essential documentation includes: * Copies of receipts or order confirmations. * Emails or chat logs with the merchant regarding the issue. * Tracking information showing a delivery to the wrong address. * A copy of the return tracking slip if you returned the item. * For fraudulent charges, you may need to file a police or FTC report.

Step 3: Understanding the Temporary Credit

Under the FCBA, while your dispute is under investigation (typically up to 90 days), Credit One will likely issue a temporary credit to your account for the disputed amount. This removes the financial burden from you during the process. Important: This credit is temporary. If the bank later determines the charge was valid, they will remove the credit and you will be responsible for the payment again.

Step 4: The Investigation and Outcome

Credit One will contact the merchant’s bank and review the evidence from both sides. They will then make a decision. You will receive a letter in the mail explaining the outcome. If the dispute is resolved in your favor, the temporary credit becomes permanent. If it is not, you have the right to ask for the documents the bank used to make its decision.

Special Considerations in the Current Landscape

Disputing Fraud vs. Billing Errors

It’s important to distinguish between the two, as the process can differ slightly. * Fraud (Unauthorized Charges): This is when someone uses your card without your permission. In this case, you should also report the card as stolen or compromised to Credit One immediately. They will close your current account and issue a new card with a new number. Liability for unauthorized charges is typically limited to $50, and often $0 if reported promptly. * Billing Errors: These are charges you authorized but are incorrect. This includes charges for the wrong amount, duplicate charges, failures to credit returns, or charges for goods that were never delivered. This is where your evidence against the merchant is key.

Disputing Subscription Services

In the age of "subscribe and save," it’s easy to forget about a free trial that converted to a paid membership. Disputing these can be tricky. If you simply forgot to cancel, the bank may side with the merchant. Your best bet is to first cancel the subscription directly through the service provider before disputing to prevent future charges.

Proactive Measures: Protecting Yourself from Future Disputes

The best dispute is the one you never have to file. * Monitor Your Account Regularly: Don’t wait for your monthly statement. Check your transactions weekly through the app or online portal. * Set Up Alerts: Use Credit One’s alert system to receive notifications for all transactions, especially those over a certain amount. * Use Virtual Card Numbers: Some financial technology apps offer virtual card numbers for online purchases, adding a layer of security. * Be Wary of Free Trials: Always read the terms and conditions and mark your calendar to cancel if you don’t want to continue. * Keep Your Records: Save receipts, order confirmations, and correspondence with merchants until the transaction appears correctly on your statement.

Navigating a charge dispute can feel daunting, but armed with the right knowledge and a methodical approach, you can confidently protect your finances. Your Credit One credit card is a powerful tool—knowing how to manage it and dispute errors ensures you remain in control.
